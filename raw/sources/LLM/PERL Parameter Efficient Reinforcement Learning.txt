Abstract
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has proven to be a strong method to align Pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) with human preferences. But training models with RLHF is computationally expensive, and an overall complex process. In this work, we study RLHF where the underlying models are trained using the parameter efficient method of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) introduced by Hu et al. [2021]. We investigate the setup of “Parameter Efficient Reinforcement Learning” (PERL), in which we perform reward model training and reinforcement learning using LoRA. We compare PERL to conventional finetuning (full-tuning) across various configurations for 7 benchmarks, including 2 novel datasets, of reward modeling and reinforcement learning. We find that PERL performs on par with the conventional RLHF setting, while training faster, and with less memory. This enables the high performance of RLHF, while reducing the computational burden that limits its adoption as an alignment technique for Large Language Models. We also release 2 novel thumbs up/down preference datasets: ‘Taskmaster Coffee’, and ‘Taskmaster Ticketing’ to promote research around RLHF.
1 Introduction
Pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 [OpenAI et al., 2023], and Gemini [Team et al., 2023, Reid et al., 2024] have shown impressive performance in a variety of tasks. Aligning these models with human preferences is key to ensuring high quality behavior [Bommasani et al., 2022]: it improves instruction following [Ouyang et al., 2022], and helps fine-tune models for behaviors which lack a direct mathematical expression of a loss function, such as safety properties [Bai et al., 2022a,b], or characteristics of summarization [Stiennon et al., 2020].
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is one of the most popular methods to achieve this alignment. It involves fitting a reward model (RM) on human preference data, and then uses this RM to tune the parameters of the LLM using Reinforcement Learning (RL). We

While RLHF has been shown to be an effective method for alignment [Stiennon et al., 2020, Bai et al., 2022b], the complexity and computational cost of its process has hindered its adoption: the RL loop, for example, requires at least twice the memory of standard fine-tuning, as it needs multiple instances of LLMs, such as a reward model and an anchor model (for KL regularization) to train properly. Another challenge to the wider adoption of RLHF lies in the difficulty to collect enough high quality training data to create an effective reward model. In this work, we investigate how Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) approaches [Houlsby et al., 2019] can make RLHF more efficient and accessible. We compare the performance of conventional RLHF, for which all the parameters of the reward model and the policy are fine-tuned, to Parameter Efficient Reinforcement Learning, referred to as PERL, which uses a PEFT method (Low-Rank Adaptation [Hu et al., 2021]) to fine-tune the reward model and an RL Policy. Our contributions in this paper are as follows:
• Through extensive experiments, we evaluate the effects of LoRA on two dimensions of the training of the RM and RL policy: model size, and number of LoRA trainable parameters.
• We compare PERL to conventional RLHF on 7 datasets, and show that PERL achieves results on par with the conventional setup which is commonly used, while being more memory efficient, and faster to train.
• We release two new preference datasets (thumbs up/down), the Taskmaster Coffee, and Ticketing datasets, which we hope will be useful when evaluating alignment methods.
2 PERL: Parameter Efficient Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
We describe how PERL makes the RLHF process more efficient. For an overview on RLHF, please refer to Appendix A.1. There are 2 model training processes as part of the RLHF process: reward model training, and reinforcement learning. We construct the PERL reward models as language models with LoRA adapters. We attach a LoRA adapter to each attention projection matrix, and only train these adapters, while keeping the language model backbone frozen. We depict this process in Figure 1.
2

Figure 2: PERL vs. Conventional Reinforcement Learning Loops
The trained LoRA adapters are saved, and combined with the projection matrices when running inference with the reward model. This one-time addition operation is performed before running the RL loop, and the resulting reward model is equivalent to a non-LoRA one. The PERL reinforcement learning loop similarly trains language models with LoRA adapters as policy models. We attach a LoRA adapter to each attention projection matrix, and only train the LoRA adapters, while keeping the language model backbone frozen. The policy is trained with the policy gradient calculated on the reward score, and a KL regularization with the anchor policy. We depict this process in Figure 2. The bulk of the memory required to train a reward model or a reinforcement learning loop is due to modern optimizers, like Adam [Kingma and Ba, 2017], or Adafactor [Shazeer and Stern, 2018], which need to track multiple factors for every trainable parameter. The reduction in the number of trainable parameters that PERL operates leads to a significant reduction in memory requirement for training. It also leads to faster training speed, because fewer parameters need to be updated at each training step.
3 Datasets and Tasks
We describe the datasets and tasks we used to train reward models and perform reinforcement learning. The datasets consist of feedback data of either preference pairs, or classification labels used to train reward models. The preference or classification labels are collected for candidate responses sampled from diverse set of policies. As it is helpful for reward models to observe as broad a domain as possible during training. In the reinforcement learning loop, we use inputs from the datasets as prompts to the LLMs to sample episodes, and make policy updates based on the reward calculated for the sampled episodes.

3.1 Text Summarization
Text summarization is one of the tasks that has been the most extensively studied using RLHF. Starting from a large text, the goal is to produce a smaller, yet faithful summary. We explore it with two different datasets: Reddit TL;DR [Völske et al., 2017], and BOLT (Broad Operational Language Translation) English SMS/Chat [Chen et al., 2018].
3.1.1 Reddit TL;DR
We experiment with Reddit post summarization, where a Reddit post is provided, and the model produces a summary of it. We base our experiments on the TL;DR dataset [Völske et al., 2017], which contains both Reddit posts, and a human preference dataset. We filtered this dataset following the work of Stiennon et al. [2020].
More details about this dataset can be found in Appendix A.2.1.
3.1.2 BOLT Message Summarization
We explore the task of summarizing sequences of messages. Such a model could be used, for instance, to show a short and accurate summary to a user, enabling them to understand the context of a conversation without reading every message in it. We experiment with the BOLT English SMS/Chat dataset [Chen et al., 2018]. This dataset was generated by paying participants to upload their conversations from SMS and chat applications, and also by randomly matching participants for conversations on a chat platform.
More details about this dataset can be found in Appendix A.3.1
3.2 Generating Harmless Responses
We explore the task of preference modeling on harmless responses by using Anthropic’s Helpfulness and Harmlessness (Red Teaming) dataset [Bai et al., 2022a]. This dataset consists of a pair of model generated responses for helpfulness examples seeking informational user queries, and harmlessness examples with users eliciting harmful responses from the model. We use the harmlessness part of the dataset to conduct our experiments. The pairwise harmlessness preference dataset labels the least harmful response generated by the model to a user elicited context.
More details about this dataset can be found in Appendix A.4.1.
3.3 Generating Helpful Responses
The Stanford Human Preferences Dataset (SHP) [Ethayarajh et al., 2022] is derived from Reddit questions/instructions, and top comments. It consists of 385,563 Reddit questions/instructions, and top-level comments for the corresponding posts. The data is split into a training set (90%), a validation set (5%), and a test set (5%). The posts are sampled from 18 domains, such as anthropology, legal advice etc.
The SHP dataset, unlike the ELI5 one [Fan et al., 2019], makes use of timestamp information to infer that a comment is preferred to another one only if it has received more votes, and has not been visible for longer (to avoid the introduction of a bias favoring older posts).
The SHP dataset differs from the Anthropic-HH dataset in that it focuses on helpfulness only (as opposed to both helpfulness and harmlessness for Anthropic-HH). The data in SHP is also human written, whereas Anthropic-HH is made of machine written responses.
Detailed information on the domains used, and how they compose the mix of examples can be found in Table 18 of Appendix A.5.1.
3.4 UI Automation
We experiment on the task of controlling a device using a UI automation dataset collected from human demonstrations. This dataset, which will be released as part of concurrent work, is composed of around 3,000 training, and 800 test episodes. Each episode is a sequence of observation and action

pairs. An observation is derived from the Android View Hierarchy, plus additional information, such as the active app, or the history of previously performed actions. An action is one of a dozen possible actions (such as clicking on a UI element, scrolling, etc.). This UI demonstration dataset only contains ground truth trajectories. We augment it to include negative samples so as to train a classifier that we use as a reward model, by replacing the ground truth action with a different, random one.
3.4.1 Training Data Preparation
We split the 8,000 sample dataset into a training set of 7,000 random examples, and a test set of 1,000 samples. These samples are generated from a randomly chosen subset of the dataset. Out of the 8,000 examples, we randomly select half of them and replace the correct action by a randomly wrong one. We call the examples leading to the right action "Positive", and the ones with a wrong action "Negative".

3.5 Generating Neutral Point of View Responses
We train a model to generate answers to controversial topics with a neutral point of view. We built a dataset consisting of 300 triplets of (question, answer, rating) spanning 93 topics. Humans trained on this task generated both questions and answers, which where then scored between 0 and 5 by high-quality annotators based on both style and neutrality. Among those 300 examples, 136 are deemed adequate answers (i.e. score >= 4). The rest of the answers’ scores spans the whole range between 0 and 4, which helps with training a classifier that we use as a reward model. The dataset was split into train, test and validation.
More information about this dataset is given in Appendix A.7.1.
3.6 Taskmaster Datasets
The Taskmaster datasets were created by paying crowdsourced (Mechanical Turk) workers to produce conversational ordering interactions using a custom-built, text-based dialogue creation tool. This meant either writing turns for both speakers, i.e. the customer and the agent, or filling in specific turns for partially completed conversations. Each conversation was annotated with function calls interleaved within the dialogue turns, signalling when to interact with external sources in order to perform tasks in the real world. Example tasks that were performed include looking up menu items or movies, adding items to an order, booking tickets, etc. The calls are API-agnostic, so they can easily be converted to any format needed when deployed. The training datasets were used to fine-tune a LaMDA model [Thoppilan et al., 2022] which was served along with an ‘API adapter’ that mapped each function call to a real API.
The trained LaMDA model was deployed to another set of crowdsourced workers who were paid to interact with it, and label its responses (conversation turns, or API calls) with thumbs up/down. We collected 3,000 (dialogue, reward) pairs in this manner, where the reward is a thumbs up/down.
More details on the data collection methodology, as well as examples and UI screenshots for creating these datasets are given in Appendix A.8.1.

3.6.1 Taskmaster Coffee
The Taskmaster Coffee dataset consists of 6,500 multi-turn conversations, consisting of 20,000 training examples (conversation turns or API calls), and 3,000 reward examples. The reward datasets contains 2,775 examples labeled with thumbs up, and 453 with thumbs down. We have publicly released this dataset and the instructions used to create it at https://github.com/google-resea rch-datasets/Taskmaster/tree/master/TM-4-2024.
3.6.2 Taskmaster Ticketing
The Taskmaster Ticketing dataset contains 30,000 multi-turn conversations, consisting of 240,000 training examples and 3,000 reward examples. The reward datasets contains 1,146 examples labeled with thumbs up, and 2,210 with thumbs down. This dataset and related materials are publicly released at https://github.com/google-research-datasets/Taskmaster/tree/master/TM-3-2 020.

6 Related Work
6.1 Pretrained Large Models
Pretrained Large Models (PLMs - [Brown et al., 2020, Smith et al., 2022, Thoppilan et al., 2022, Workshop et al., 2022, Hoffmann et al., 2022, Chowdhery et al., 2022, Touvron et al., 2023, Anil et al., 2023, OpenAI et al., 2023, Jiang et al., 2023]) have shown strong performance in a variety of tasks. They have been used in many different applications, such as summarization [Stiennon et al., 2020], instruction following [Ouyang et al., 2022, Lai et al., 2023], conversational recommender systems [Friedman et al., 2023], question answering [Nakano et al., 2022], personalized dialogue generation [Jandaghi et al., 2023], text annotation tasks [Gilardi et al., 2023], audio generation [Borsos et al., 2023], robotics tasks [Driess et al., 2023], vision tasks [Chen et al., 2023], and more [Ziegler et al., 2020]. Pretrained [Radford et al., 2018, Ramachandran et al., 2016], and instruction-tuned [Wei et al., 2021] large models, while demonstrating impressive performance on a multitude of tasks, still exhibit many limitations, such as returning non-factual outputs, or not following human instructions correctly.
6.2 Aligning Pretrained Large Models with Human/AI Preferences
Aligning Pretrained Large Models with human preferences has become an important area of research to overcome some of their limitations [Christiano et al., 2017, Leike et al., 2018, Wang et al., 2023, Ji et al., 2023], and is also a paradigm commonly used to steer the behavior of PLMs towards human desiderata. This alignment is generally achieved by collecting a large dataset of contexts, pairwise generations for these contexts, and related preferences. Overfitting the reward function can be an important issue when approximating it [Azar et al., 2023], and methods like early stopping, or reducing the number of trainable parameters like we do in this work can help mitigate this issue, and train better policies.
While most alignment techniques have so far been used on natural language tasks, some recent work has started to apply them on other modalities as well [Lee et al., 2023b, Sun et al., 2023].
The size and quality of the human preference data can often be a bottleneck to this alignment process. For that reason, different approaches leveraging AI feedback [Bai et al., 2022b, Lee et al., 2023a] have been developed, such as Generator-Critic architectures [Jandaghi et al., 2023, Yuan et al., 2024].
Different techniques have been developed to align PLMs with human/AI preferences, including Reward rAnked Fine-Tuning (RAFT) [Dong et al., 2023], RRHF [Yuan et al., 2023], Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [Christiano et al., 2017, Ouyang et al., 2022, Azar et al., 2023], Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [Rafailov et al., 2023, Azar et al., 2023], Sequence Likelihood Calibration with Human Feedback (SLIC-HF) [Zhao et al., 2023], Pairwise Cringe Optimization [Xu et al., 2023a], and Self-Rewarding Language Models [Yuan et al., 2024].
Among these methods, RL{H/AI}F is one of the most popular. As we show here, by combining RL{H/AI}F with parameter-efficient methods like LoRA, the compute and memory efficiencies of the model tuning can be improved substantially.
6.3 Reinforcement Learning from Human/AI Feedback (RL{H/AI}F)
Reinforcement Learning has been shown to be a useful technique to align agents with human preferences [Dewey, 2011, Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016, Everitt and Hutter, 2018]. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) works by first fitting a reward model on the preferred outputs. This reward model is then used to train a policy using a reinforcement learning algorithm, typically Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms (PPO) [Schulman et al., 2017].
Labeling examples to train a reward model can be costly [Casper et al., 2023], and many of the existing works had to use tens of thousands of comparison examples to obtain good reward models to train the RL loop from [Stiennon et al., 2020, Menick et al., 2022]. To alleviate this labour intensive process, some work has been replacing human feedback by AI feedback, leading to RLAIF approaches [Bai et al., 2022b, Kim et al., 2023, Lee et al., 2023a]). [Lee et al., 2023a] show that RLHF and RLAIF can have comparable performance.
14

6.4 Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning and Low-Rank Adaptation
Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods [Houlsby et al., 2019, Ding et al., 2022, Xu et al., 2023b] reduce the number of trainable parameters of a Pretrained Large Model, while maintaining comparable performance to full fine-tuning. These methods are useful to adapt a PLM to downstream tasks: full fine-tuning on these tasks necessitates large amounts of labeled data to avoid overfitting, and significant compute resources. PEFT methods leverage the knowledge already acquired by a PLM, and can adapt a small number of parameters on a relatively small dataset. Hugging Face’s PEFT library is an example implementation of PEFT methods [HuggingFace, 2023a].
Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [Hu et al., 2021] is an example PEFT method, and the one that we used in our experiments. It factorizes the weight update into two trainable, low rank matrices (down-projection, and up-projection). During training, only these low rank matrices are being updated. Depending on the rank of the decomposition, we end up training only a small fraction of the total number of parameters of the original model. Similarly to the original LoRA paper, we apply the low rank projections to the attention layers, but we note that other layers can be used. LoRA offers the advantages that it doesn’t increase the inference time (unlike adapters [Houlsby et al., 2019], for example, which deepen the model), nor does it reduce the context window (unlike prompt-tuning [Lester et al., 2021], for which some tokens must be used for the tunable prompts). Hydra-RLHF [Santacroce et al., 2023] achieves memory savings in RLHF by sharing a backbone, similarly to our work. They do not, however, compare Hydra-RLHF to fully tuned RLHF. QLoRA [Dettmers et al., 2023] combines quantization, and diverse optimizations with LoRA to achieve memory efficient fine-tuning. These optimizations can be incorporated into our method to lead to further memory gains.
6.5 Infrastructure
The Transformer Reinforcement Learning (TRL) library [von Werra et al., 2020] is the closest implementation of Parameter Efficient Reinforcement Learning that we are aware of. Their MultiAdapter RL (MARL) approach uses a shared base model for the entire RLHF pipeline [HuggingFace, 2024]. This feature, however, is experimental and has not been tested yet. Besides, and to the best of our knowledge, the TRL library has only been used in combination with their Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) library on [HuggingFace, 2023a,b] in the single GPU setting, and it only supports transformer models for text. In contrast, our implementation is parallelized, and generalizes to modalities other than text.
We implemented our work in Python using the PAX library [Paxml, 2022]. Our input and evaluation pipeline relies on the SeqIO library [Roberts et al., 2022]. We used an internally designed RL training loop infrastructure.
7 Conclusion and Future Work
We have presented PERL, a Parameter Efficient Reinforcement Learning technique that can train a Reward Model and RL tune a Language Model Policy with LoRA. Through extensive experiments on various datasets, we have shown that this method achieves comparable results to conventional RLHF, for which all the model parameters are tuned, while reducing memory usage by approx 50%, and speeding up the training by up to 90% for the Reward Model training, and more modest memory savings of 20%, and speed-up of 10% in the RL loop.
As we demonstrate the success of the PERL method, there are still some questions that are underexplored in our study. We are planning to investigate the following avenues as part of our future work:
• PERL matches the performance on in-domain held out test sets, but the exploration of broader generalization would be interesting. There is room to explore ensemble models like Mixture-of-LoRA adapters [Zhu et al., 2023] for the RLHF process at a minimal computational cost that will introduce the robustness needed in training to facilitate crossdomain generalization.
• Reward models are one of the most critical pieces of the RLHF process, but they are prone to the “reward hacking” behavior. Recent works like Ramé et al. [2024] show that weight-
15

averaging models mitigate reward hacking. It would be interesting to explore this with PERL, as it can provide similar benefits by weight-averaging multiple adapters at a much lower computational cost.
Ethical Considerations
To create the Taskmaster Coffee dataset, crowdsourced (Mechanical Turk) workers were paid to create task-based conversational dialogues of about 6-8 turns, each taking roughly three minutes or less to complete. At the time of collection, the project drew from a pool of more than 1,000 U.S.based workers who had shown proficiency in previous dialogue writing tasks. None are personally identifiable, as is standard on the MTurk platform.

A Appendix

A.1 RLHF Preliminaries
We review the RLHF pipeline introduced in Stiennon et al. [2020], which consists of 3 phases: supervised fine-tuning, reward model training, and reinforcement learning.

A.1.1 Supervised Fine-tuning
This is the most common type of tuning done to enable LLMs to cater to specific tasks, like summarization, dialogue generation, etc. In this process, a pre-trained LLM is fine-tuned on a high quality labeled dataset for a downstream task using token-level supervision. We refer to such a model using πAnchor.

A.1.2 Reward Modeling
To perform reinforcement learning, we need a reward score for each episode or response generation. We leverage a model-based solution for this.
A popular approach consists of deriving this reward from preferences pairs. In this preference pair comparison formulation, we learn a reward from preferences expressed over pairs of candidate responses. Given an input x, we sample a pair of responses (y1, y2) ∼ π from one or more models. We collect preference labels over the candidates from humans. We can also collect these labels by prompting an LLM model as shown by Lee et al. [2023a]. These labels form a preference pair dataset of triplets D = {(x, yw, yl)}, where yw is the preferred response, and yl is the non-preferred one, given input x. A reward model (RM) rϕ is trained according to the Bradley-Terry-Luce Model [Bradley and Terry, 1952], which assumes that label yw is preferred over label yl with probability rϕ(x,yw)+rϕ(x,yl) rϕ(x,yw) , by minimizing the following loss:

Lr(ϕ) = −E

log σ rϕ(x, yw) − rϕ(x, yl) ,

(x,yw ,yl)∼D

where σ is the sigmoid function.
Other popular ways to train a reward model include learning classifiers to produce rewards between 0 and 1, or regression models predicting reward scores close to the label rating collected over context response tuples.

A.1.3 Reinforcement Learning
We optimize a policy πRL θ with reinforcement learning to maximize the cumulative reward given by a reward model. The weights of the policy are initialized from those of the SFT model. A Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence term DKL is added to the objective to penalize πRL θ for deviating too much from the initial anchor policy πAnchor. This term is controlled by the hyperparameter β [Fox et al., 2015, Geist et al., 2019]. Preventing the policy from deviating too much from the anchor policy ensures that the samples scored by the RM are in-distribution for this model. This prevents πRL θ from drifting into a region where it generates language that is highly rewarded by the RM yet is low-quality or gibberish, for example (a phenomenon known as “reward hacking” [Everitt and Hutter, 2016, Amodei et al., 2016]).
The optimization objective is described by the equation below:

J (θ) = E (1 − β)rϕ(y|x) − βDKL πθRL(y|x) || πAnchor(y|x) ,
y∼πθ (·|x)
where β is a hyperparameter in the range [0,1]. 26

A.2 Reddit TL;DR Summarization
A.2.1 Dataset
We use OpenAI’s human preference dataset introduced in Stiennon et al. [2020] in the RL for summarization task. This dataset of preference pairs is created from the filtered Reddit TL;DR dataset constructed by Völske et al. [2017]. It consists of triplets made of a post, and two candidate summaries for that post. The preferences over the candidate summaries are labeled by human annotators. The dataset contains a total of 92,000 pairwise comparisons.
A.2.2 Hyperparameter Tuning
We train all reward modeling setups using a batch size of 128. We train all the reward models for 5,000 steps, and pick the checkpoint with highest validation pairwise accuracy. We consider learning rates from [1e-5, 5e-5, 1e-4, 2e-4, 5e-4]. We find that the best learning rate in full-tuning setups is 1e-5, and the best learning rate in LoRA training setups is 1e-4. We conduct the RL training using the "REINFORCE for Language Models" algorithm used by Lee et al. [2023a]. We sample 128 episodes per batch using the policy with temperature of 0.7 for decoding. We use β = 0.05 for KL regularization. We set the learning rate to 1e-5 for the reinforcement learning of the policy model. We picked this learning rate after trying 1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-5, 5e-5, and 1e-6.
A.3 Message Summarization
A.3.1 Dataset
We use the BOLT English SMS/Chat dataset [Chen et al., 2018] for the task of RL for summarizing sequences of chat messages. Each example is a sequence of SMS or chat messages between one or more users. All examples are in English. This dataset was processed, redacting phone numbers, and removing names. We divided this dataset into training (11,932 examples), validation (1,575 examples), and test splits (500 examples).
A.3.2 Hyperparameter Tuning
We sample 128 episodes using the policy with temperature of 0.9 for decoding per batch. We use β = 0.01 for KL regularization. We set the learning rate to 1e-5 for the policy model.
A.4 Anthropic-HH Dataset
A.4.1 Dataset
We use the Helpfulness and Harmlessness dataset introduced by Anthropic [2022] to train a reward model. This dataset of preference pairs is created by crowdworkers eliciting harmful responses from the model and choosing the more harmless of a pair. The model used to elicit responses from is a 52B context-distilled LM. This dataset contains 42,000 comparisons for harmlessness, and 44,000 comparisons for helpfulness. Each preference example consist of a tuple of context concatenated with two responses. We based our experiments on the harmfulness split, and have not experimented with the helpfulness one.
A.4.2 Hyperparameter Tuning
We train all reward modeling setups using a batch size of 128. We train all the reward models for 5K steps, and pick the checkpoint with highest validation pairwise accuracy. We consider learning rates from [1e-5, 5e-5, 1e-4, 2e-4, 5e-4]. We find that the best learning rate in full-tuning setups is 1e-5 and the best learning rate in LoRA training setups is 2e-4.

A.8 Taskmaster Datasets
A.8.1 Datasets
Following Byrne et al. [2021], the Taskmaster datasets were created using the ‘self-dialogue’ technique: a single, crowdsourced worker is paid to create a conversation which may include writing turns for both speakers, i.e. the customer and the agent.
Figure 6 shows a data sample from the original ticketing dataset.
In order to gather a wide variety of conversational scenarios and linguistic phenomena, workers were encouraged to reflect on the given scenario first, and think about how the conversation would unfold in a spoken dialogue before writing an utterance that best fits the situation, as shown in Figure 7.
For the majority of this dataset, the workers were given a conversation in which some turns are already filled in, and were instructed to complete the conversation based on the specific context in the dialogue.
We used 30 sets of instructions to build this corpus. In addition to filling out the conversation turns, workers were also asked to choose menu items and other entities ahead of time. For example, Figure 8 shows a text field below the coffee menu where the worker entered their choice. This entity was stored as a variable, and could be accurately referenced both in conversational utterances and in the function calls, as seen in Figure 7.
As discussed earlier, the training dataset was used to fine-tune a LaMDA model [Thoppilan et al., 2022] for raters to evaluate, and each rating was then exported as a training example for the reward dataset. Figure 9 shows an example of a model response in the rater UI, where candidates for a given turn are marked as either thumbs-up or thumbs-down based on the context.
