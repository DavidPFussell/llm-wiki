THE PROMISE OF GENERATIVE AI IS REAL
1 "Bullshit Jobs: A Theory" by David Graeber.

To this day, much of the work done by employees in an enterprise is drudge work. Or— as anthropologist David Graeber called them — "bullshit jobs."1 It needs to be done, but it does not take advantage of the creativity, intelligence, and judgment that remains unique to humans.
Automating these tasks through the use of Generative AI — and extending those approaches to countless value-adding tasks that are not being done — is a win for both employees and the companies that they work for.
It’s imperative that you — and your colleagues, teams, and leaders — understand that when building a Generative AI strategy, it’s not just about chatbots.
It’s about building human-like intelligence into thousands of processes throughout your business.
This transformation isn’t solely about cost cutting, but unlocking new revenue opportunities and improved customer experience that would have been unthinkable due to the high cost of human knowledge work. This has been the vision of AI developers for decades — this latest generation of models creates the opportunity for that vision to become a reality.

THE HYPE IS NOT GOING AWAY
Given the amount of attention that Generative AI has been receiving in the popular and business media, executives are rightly wondering whether this is yet another technology hype cycle that will fizzle in the coming months.
It is not. Given that the capabilities of these models far surpass what even their developers could have expected (more on this in the next section), it is right to see these models as ushering us into a new era.
That said, we are still early in the adoption of Generative AI in the enterprise. Enterprises are rightly risk-averse — adopting a new technology whose performance and capabilities we don’t yet fully understand is to be met with caution, to be sure. And outside of the companies developing these models themselves, businesses have not yet been transformed by this technology (though that will quickly change).

What Makes Generative AI Different?
Machine learning and artificial intelligence systems have been under development and in productive use for decades (even centuries if you count the systems that predate modern computers). As it happens, the key technology behind Generative AI, known as transformer models, has been around since 2017.
But the current models that have gained attention — increasingly known as foundation models because they can be used as the “foundation” for building other systems — are different. First, they are different in how they are built. Using years of research into neural networks and powered by ever more available cloud computing resources, these models are very large and use a new architecture.
A big reason foundation models are poised to democratize AI is because they are pre-trained — the ability to use smaller datasets with these models is game changing.

Today’s foundation models, like those that power popular chatbots like ChatGPT, Bard, and Bing, are also different in how they perform. For reasons that are not entirely understood, these models have gained new abilities with their larger size. The models that we have today are able to:
• Generate text that reads as if it was written by a human, adapting their tone and level of sophistication according to their instructions (and in a great number of languages).
• Analyze very complex text and extract information or summarize the text in a way that a human would agree accurately captures the main ideas and essential details.
• Outperform traditional natural language processing (NLP) models in areas that those smaller models were built to perform in, like detecting the sentiment of text or identifying the people or topics being discussed (called “entity extraction”).

These examples are all text-related, but it’s worth noting that non-language media like images, video, etc., is also on the table and is following closely behind text capabilities with enterprises already experimenting with the automated generation of fully customized marketing copy and images, for example.
It’s also worth noting that unlike machine learning models, these models are not deterministic (i.e., they do not produce the same result every time for a set of inputs). They are stochastic, meaning there is a certain degree of randomness in the outcomes. This presents challenges for using them reliably and consistently in certain enterprise applications.

The 2 Tiers of Generative AI Applications
If the first generations of AI were like the steam power of the first industrial revolution, Generative AI models are the electricity of the second industrial revolution — they will be transformative in ways that we do not yet anticipate.
Therefore, it is useful to think about potential applications for this technology in terms of how they will be transformative:
• Moonshots: Generative AI that is used for a few fundamentally new capabilities in the business so powerful that these applications transform the business directly.
• Mundane: Generative AI that is used to augment hundreds or thousands of processes and decisions throughout the business.

THINK MUNDANE AND MOONSHOT TO SUCCEED

The most commonly imagined moonshot application of Generative AI in the enterprise is the all-knowing, oracular chatbot. This always-on, alwaysaccurate assistant can provide immediate answers about the current state of the business as well as make accurate predictions about the future. Could this work? Potentially, but there are many caveats and many reasons to doubt that such a system would ever be fully trusted.
Receiving less attention is the potential for Generative AI to transform the business through the augmentation of countless mundane tasks. In this scenario, the business chooses different models for different applications, balancing considerations like performance, cost, and privacy. Teams are empowered to apply approved technologies to the challenges that they face. The technology is democratized, people are empowered, and there is a massive increase in productivity thanks to teams across the business being able to build solutions to their own challenges.
You need to be able to do both.
Yes, you should start building the rocket for that moonshot. But even more immediately, you must start augmenting processes throughout your organization, finding the right balance between autonomy and control.

How to Put Generative AI to Work
Generative AI becomes even more valuable to companies when combined with other organizational assets — i.e., connected to enterprise systems in a way that is safe and reliable. Specifically, Generative AI needs to be combined with:

ENTERPRISE DATA
By “fine tuning” an LLM or other Generative AI model on proprietary data, the model can provide relevant
and accurate responses specific to the business. The applications are potentially wide ranging, from automating document review to creating sophisticated knowledge
management systems.

IMPROVED PROMPTING
We are discovering how these models work and respond to different ways of providing them with prompts. By devising systems to assist people in sending better prompts, the models
can be more valuable assistants. Those prompting best practices need to then be industrialized within the
enterprise.

PREDICTIVE MODELS
LLMs are not good at the math and statistics required to build predictive
models. By combining an LLM’s language generation capabilities with the solid math of a predictive model,
you can provide highly customized content that drives behavior.

HUMAN VALIDATION & FEEDBACK
The models will not always be right, and their responses may not all be appropriate. Providing this feedback in a controlled manner will improve the model and create a way to capture institutional knowledge in a scalable
way.

EXAMPLE: ULTRA-CUSTOM MARKETING AUTOMATION

• Before Generative AI A predictive model generates a product recommendation based on the customer’s known demographic details and purchasing history. These are promoted via templated email, but often get caught in spam filters or ignored by consumers due to their scripted tone.
• With Generative AI The same product recommendation is now embedded in a hyperpersonalized, multi-lingual, always-unique conversation with the customer. For example, a fashion retailer could build a virtual personal stylist for each customer, each with an appropriate tone and style. The customer can interact with the virtual stylist, building a rapport and driving sales.
• Don’t Forget! Hyper-personalized content will come off double poorly if you send it to the wrong person, so double check you have the right business involvement in your segment building. Live test this kind of solution with employees, and only then with trusted customers, to make the right kinds of headlines.

EXAMPLE: AUTOMATED CONTRACT INTELLIGENCE

• Before Generative AI Organizations seeking to optimize their sales and purchasing build rules-based analyses combining various NLP techniques to index contracts and learn about their business. The variability of the contract language and format frustrates this process, leading to complex, custom rules that are fragile and require ongoing maintenance by specialists.
• With Generative AI Organizations can use an LLM to automate the extraction of important contract clauses, creating structured, tabular data out of chaotic documents. From there, they can automate the computation of, for example, compounded financial risks. Previously, this data would have gone untapped due to the need for the prohibitively expensive, specialized skills of a lawyer to find it and extract it. LLMs create the possibility to scale that specialized knowledge.
• Don’t Forget! It’s still not possible to send AI to jail for mistakes. Ensure the human who is accountable for these decisions is also responsible and checks the outputs rigorously, both to improve the system and reduce risk.

EXAMPLE: PREDICTIVE MAINTENANCE BUDDY

• Before Generative AI A predictive maintenance model generates a list of machines in a factory to prioritize for inspection for a day. This is given to the factory floor technician as a static report on a clipboard or a digital device, who then inspects the machine but lacks context and an immediate feedback loop.
• With Generative AI The list of reports is conveyed dynamically throughout the day to the technician over walkie-talkie using voice generation. The technician can keep their eyes on their environment, increasing safety. The channel is twoway: the technician can respond and ask additional questions. The “buddy” can also share other information, e.g., health and safety messages based on the day’s weather, the overall workload, or other relevant dimensions. The tone and language can be customized, supporting a diverse, multilingual workforce.
• Don’t Forget! When it comes to safety, noisy signals are a silent killer. Remember that such technicians will have been approached many times in their career with “the latest and greatest” only to see it never quite add value, only distraction. To ensure the walkie-talkie doesn’t get switched off, build out such a product with a group of shop-floor technicians, prioritizing the features that will ease adoption.

EXAMPLE: CALL CENTER COACHING

• Before Generative AI Companies with call center operations struggle to ensure that the quality of the many individual interactions remains consistently high. Agents are coached sporadically, with only a small fraction of customer interactions being reviewed.
• With Generative AI An LLM can review the transcripts of all conversations, providing customized coaching for each agent based on the company’s documented guidelines and best practices. Any concerning interactions could be flagged and sent to management for review.
• Don’t Forget! Nobody likes the feeling of someone watching them work over their shoulder. Such a system should be built collaboratively with the agents that it is meant to benefit to ensure that it is received as a useful helper, not an intrusive surveillance system.

Achieving Generative AI at Scale
While it may prove to be possible for an organization to succeed in its moonshot and build a single chatbot that is accurate, safe, and costeffective, serving the needs of the entire organization, this must not be an organization’s sole Generative AI strategy. It is too risky, and it misses the myriad improvements that are much closer at hand.
How, then, can an organization succeed in scaling the use of Generative AI throughout their organization? There are three essential pillars to achieving this scale:

1 Democratization: There is a massive gap between the potential applications for Generative AI in a large enterprise and the number of experts who could potentially build those systems. More people need to be involved, and the application of Generative AI to business problems needs to be democratized.
2 Acceleration: Competitive pressure means that time is of the essence. Every step in the development and deployment process needs to be accelerated. Importantly, assets need to be reused and repurposed throughout the organization — you cannot start from scratch for every new application of the technology.
3 Trust: For Generative AI to truly serve the needs of the business, it needs to be trusted by its beneficiaries and to not expose the organization to additional risks.

PATHWAYS FOR GENERATIVE AI AT SCALE
There are several pathways to scaling Generative AI (as well as traditional AI and machine learning). In choosing the correct pathway, executives will need to weigh the tradeoffs between:
• Short-term time-to-value • Long-term return on investment (ROI), including technical debt • Up-front versus ongoing costs • Available resources, including existing skills within the organization • Current technology assets and medium-term roadmap • Budget

Like other technology initiatives, four main pathways are available:
1 Services Outsourcing the development and deployment of the AI capabilities to a consulting or systems integration services provider.
2 Point Solutions Purchasing dedicated, AI-powered point solutions to augment individual operations and processes throughout the organization.
3 Do-it-Yourself Developing the internal AI and software development capabilities to build custom solutions throughout the organization.
4 Platform Investing in an AI development platform, like Dataiku, to empower teams to build AI into their operations throughout the organization.

EXPLORING THE SERVICES-FIRST PATH
The service providers that organizations have worked with for decades are increasingly offering services to build Generative AI-powered capabilities for their enterprise customers.

PROS
• Solutions built through a services engagement can be highly customized to the specifics of your technology and operations.
• Service providers can provide scarce expertise that might be absent in your organization.
• Established relationships with consultancies and system integrators (SIs) means work can start quickly.

CONS
• An ongoing relationship is required to maintain existing solutions and develop new applications when the need is identified, creating a dependency on an external organization.
• The knowledge required to develop the solutions is not developed within the enterprise, creating an ongoing reliance on an external service provider for what will likely become a core strategic initiative.
• Total costs can be high (and high costs might bias project selection toward complex use cases more likely to fail).

EXPLORING THE POINT SOLUTION PATH
As the attention on Generative AI increases, ever more startups will develop AI-powered solutions solving specific problems in the organization (e.g., AI-powered email generation for sales development representatives, AI-powered contract review for purchasing, etc.).

PROS
• The models developed can offer very high performance in their specific area.
• In many cases, they can offer rapid time to value, as they can be used nearly off the shelf.

CONS
• Point solutions, by definition, are not scalable. They augment one process, but do not provide any benefit to adjacent processes.
• Technical debt accumulates as more point solutions become part of critical business processes, creating dependencies on external vendors.
• Buying off-the-shelf solutions does not help develop the core skills necessary to develop Generative AI-powered capabilities throughout the organization.
• The beneficiaries in the business may not trust the results of the AI system if they perceive it was purchased to change how they work without their involvement (especially if it’s a black-box system).

EXPLORING THE DO-IT-YOURSELF PATH
The technology teams in companies of all types have become increasingly sophisticated as they have faced successive waves of innovation. These teams may be able to build Generative AI-powered solutions, combining open-source software with components provided by cloud computing partners.

PROS
• The solutions developed will be well-integrated into the company’s IT stack and will often be highly customized to the needs of the business.
• Developing solutions in-house will preserve maximum IP in the company.

CONS
• The skills needed to develop Generative AI-powered solutions are scarce and expensive. Many traditional businesses face challenges recruiting these profiles who are in demand at technology companies.
• Developing more Generative AI-powered capabilities internally will gradually add additional maintenance requirements, reaching a point where little to no time can be spent on creating new capabilities.
• The skills needed to develop and maintain robust systems is not the core expertise of many traditional businesses. Not every company needs to be a software company, and almost no companies have the resources to truly develop novel foundation models.

EXPLORING THE PLATFORM PATH
Over the last decade, software platforms have emerged that are designed to allow enterprises to build machine learning and AI capabilities into their business. Dataiku is the established leader in this category.

PROS
• Modern AI platforms lower the technical barriers to entry, allowing a far wider range of employees to participate in the development and deployment of AI capabilities, bringing domain expertise deeper into the process.
• A common platform that is used across the organization fosters the reuse of data and AI assets, ensuring that teams aren’t constantly reinventing the wheel when starting a new project.
• A common platform allows for an AI governance policy to be established and enforced, reducing the risk associated with the broad scaling of AI throughout the organization.

CONS
• A platform that enables collaboration between different parts of the organization that have previously worked in separate tools will require a willingness for organizational change.
• The platform features that accelerate common tasks may limit the flexibility to adapt to edge cases. This can be mitigated by selecting a highly extensible platform, like Dataiku, that allows less common tasks to be performed using common coding languages.
• Some platforms may create the risk of vendor lock-in, though best-inbreed options like Dataiku mitigate this risk through the use of open standards and exportability.

Why a Platform Is the Key to Scaling Generative AI

Making an informed decision on incorporating Generative AI into your operations from the beginning is critical as it will impact performance for years to come.
For nearly all organizations, investing in an AI platform will be the best choice for the simple reason that it allows you to take the best aspects of the other three possible paths, all while also addressing their shortcomings.
With a platform:
• Service providers can still provide staff augmentation, working in the platform to ensure that their deliverables are leveraged and maintained after the end of their engagement.
• Point solutions can be integrated when they provide an incremental benefit for a particular application, while maintaining an overall governance structure across all AI initiatives.
• The highly technical builders within the organization will see their work accelerated, while allowing them to collaborate more closely with their business beneficiaries.
