SUMMARY
Cross-validatory estimation of the bilinear model based on principal components is reviewed and Krzanowski’s modification of Wold’s procedure is described. Two different types of residuals useful for checking model adequacy are defined and indices measuring the influence of each observed unit on the estimates of the parameters are discussed. A method for the selection of variabIes derived from Procrustes analysis is described. Results arising from the study of two sets of enological data are given.

INTRODUCTION
Modern instrumental methods of chemical analysis of foods enable the concentration of a very high number of components to be determined accurately and simultaneously. Thus an extensive chemical characterization of samples takes, at present, a matter of hours. Frequently, however, as a consequence of a thorough survey, redundant variables are included in the observed data and useful information can be partly obscured by data variability and distorted by outliers, so that statistical methods are required to retain ‘structure’ and discard ‘noise’.
Customary problems to be solved at the very beginning of an investigation are (i) the recognition of influential observations, (ii) the determination of the data dimensionality and (iii) the identification of ‘important’ variables containing most of the information supplied by the data. Influential observations, by definition, cause considerable changes in the results of a statistical analysis when they are discarded from the data. Influence analysis’ can also be of use in recognizing untypical cases and/or outliers. Depending on the structure of the data, the assessment of the dimensionality is carried out by principal components analysis (PCA) or

Selection of variables in PCA
PCA is often used as a dimension-reducing technique, i.e. to determine q 4 p new variables containing most of the information supplied by the data. However, each principal component is a linear combination of the observed variables and then, even if the data dimensionality can be reduced from p to q, all the observed variables are again necessary to derive the new ones. On the contrary, in the applications one wants to reduce not only the space dimension but also the number of variables that should be observed in the future.
Pioneering work on this subject has been done by J 0 1 l i f f e ~a~nd~ McCabe.6 More recently, Krzanowski’ suggested the use of Procrustes analysis” to determine subsets of the observed variables reproducing as closely as possible the main features of the data reflected by the selected q principal components.
Krzanowski’s method
Let us suppose that the data dimensionality is q < p . We denote with CXthe n x q matrix of
the first q principal components of X. For any given subset of k observed variables, with
q < k < p , let Y be the corresponding n x k data matrix and let CY be the n x q matrix whose
column vectors are the first q principal components of Y . Geometrically, the rows of CY can be thought of as an n-point configuration in q-dimensional space which approximates the ‘true’
configuration associated to CX.Obviously, the approximation is due to the fact that CYis based on k < p variables. T o evaluate the discrepancy between the configurations of CX and CY, Krzanowski suggests the use of Procrustes T’-statistics, which is equal to the minimum value
attainable by the sum of the squared distances between the corresponding points of the two
configurations when one of these, e.g. CY,is matched to the other by translations, rotations
and reflections. The optimal translation is that which superimposes the centroids of the two configurations.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Polyphenol data
Polyphenol substances predominantly contribute to the organoleptic characteristics of red wines, mainly the taste, the colour and, in part, the aroma.
A number of measurements concerning the content of polyphenol substances were made on 22 samples of three celebrated Italian red wines, Chianti Classico (five samples, 1982 vintage), Brunello di Montalcino (five samples, 1982 vintage) and Marzemino del Trentino (twelve samples, 1976 vintage). Chianti and Brunello wines are produced in contiguous areas in Tuscany (central Italy); Marzemino wine is produced in the Trentino region (north Italy). The Chianti wine is obtained from mixed grapes with a 50% minimum of the ‘Sangiovese’ variety, while the Brunello wine is produced from the grape variety ‘Sangiovese grosso’. Thus these two wines are expected not to be very different.
Samples were analysed to determine20p21total phenols (two different methods), polymerizable phenols, antocyanins and leucoantocyanins. The five different measurements were made (a) on untreated wines, (b) on wines treated with cinchonine sulphate (substances with tannic properties removed by precipitation), (c) on wines treated with sodium chloride (polymeric substances of between six and ten units removed by precipitation) and (d) on the extract obtained with ethyl acetate on NaC1-treated wines (only polyphenols with a low degree of polymerization or monomer are extracted). Overall means, minimum and maximum values for each type of wine and the between-groups variance to total variance ratios are given in Table 1 together with a brief description of the observed variables.
Preliminary analyses showed two main features of the data. A number of variables have notably different distributions for the three types of wine. In particular, as shown by the variance ratios in the fourth column of Table 1, almost all of the variability of the leucoantocyanins (E-variables) arises from the differences between the means of the three wines. Moreover, pairwise linear correlations (see Table 2) are generally high, e.g. corr(A1, B1) = corr(E1, E3) = 0-99, corr(A3, B3) = 0.97 and corr(A4, B4) = 0.96. However, the polymerizable phenols (C-variables), excluding C1, have comparatively low correlations with the other variables.

CONCLUDING REMARKS
The bilinear model based on principal components accomplishes two main tasks: it deepens our knowledge of the structure of multivariate data and at the same time it can be used as a prediction tool. However, some difficulties accompany its use, above all the determination of the number of factors to be entered in the model. Cross-validatory estimation is no doubt better than methods of a more subjective nature such as the percentage trace criterion and the rule based on the ‘least greater than one’ eigenvalue (when working with standardized data). Nevertheless, statistics such as the W-ratio can be difficult to interpret because of negative values and non-monotonicity. Another problem is the absence of distributional results and thus of significance tests. To solve this latter problem in the general small-sample case, a distribution (or at least some properties involving the covariance matrix) for the random component of the model should be described. At present, in the standard least squares setting, this distribution is left completely undetermined.
Residual analysis and influence analysis deserve routine use, in the first case to check model fitting, in the second to ensure that the results are not dominated by a single observation. As shown in this work, these diagnostics can be of use in revealing gross errors in the data and extreme samples. The open questions include the properties of the different types of residuals, the definition of probabilistic or at least empirical criteria to settle when influence is large, and the generalization to groups of influential observations.
The screening of important variables is an old problem; now it is becoming important in chemometrics because of the increased use of semi-automatic instruments able to measure several tens of variables on the same sample. Procrustes analysis is a general method for comparing a test configuration with a reference configuration, and the previous examples showed that it provides sensible solutions to the variable selection problem. The function which
summarizes the discrepancy between the configurations (the Procrustes statistics T2)stresses
the geometrical aspects of the matching, while the criteria suggested by Jolliffe and McCabe are based on the correlation structure of the variables. Thus we expect that they can select quite different subsets with respect to the Procrustes procedure. We hope shortly to give a comparison of these methods based on real data.
